indications:
  - Acute diarrhea, vomiting, or any illness with fluid loss
  - Suspected hypovolemia in community or ward settings
  - Reassessment after rehydration has started
equipment:
  - Vital signs and a reliable history of intake and output when available
  - A structured bedside sequence for general, circulatory, and urine-output assessment
  - Weights or fluid-balance data if locally available
keySteps:
  - Assess overall appearance, mental status, and hemodynamic stability first
  - Look for clinical clues such as tachycardia, dry mucosa, poor skin turgor, and reduced urine output in context
  - Decide whether oral replacement is safe or escalation is needed
  - Reassess after treatment rather than assuming the first estimate stays true
pitfalls:
  - Waiting for lab tests before estimating severity
  - Treating one sign in isolation as definitive
  - Forgetting to reassess after fluids start

## Practical frame

Students should treat dehydration assessment as a repeated bedside judgment, not as a one-time label.
